Attack angle is one of the numbers your launch monitor reports about the club, not the ball. It describes how the club head was traveling at the moment it struck the ball.

## The definition

Attack angle is the angle of the club head's path at impact, measured relative to level ground.

- **Negative attack angle.** The club is moving downward at impact. The lowest point of the swing arc is past the ball, in front of it.
- **Zero attack angle.** The club is moving level at impact. The lowest point is at the ball.
- **Positive attack angle.** The club is moving upward at impact. The lowest point is behind the ball, before the strike.

The number is reported in degrees. A reading of "-3" means the club was traveling 3 degrees downward through the ball. A reading of "+2" means it was traveling 2 degrees upward.

## Why it varies by club

Attack angle is not a single right number for all clubs. It varies because different clubs are designed to do different jobs.

**Irons and wedges** are typically struck with a descending blow. The ball is sitting on the ground (or on a low tee, in some cases), and the geometry of an iron's loft and shaft means the club moves down and slightly forward through impact, then continues into the ground after the ball. That is why irons and wedges leave divots, and why the ball gets pinched between the club face and the turf, generating spin.

A typical iron attack angle is somewhere in the negative range. The exact number varies by player and club.

**Drivers** are typically struck off a tee, with the ball sitting well above the ground. The geometry is reversed: the lowest point of the driver swing tends to be behind the ball, and the club is on the way back up by the time it reaches the tee. That is a positive attack angle.

A typical driver attack angle is in the positive range, though some players hit driver with a negative attack angle and still produce good results.

**Fairway woods and hybrids** sit somewhere in the middle. The ball is on the ground or on a very low tee, but the club's design encourages a sweeping strike rather than a steep descending one.

The point is not that there is a "correct" attack angle for each club. The point is that attack angle differs by club because the design and the strike conditions differ.

## How a launch monitor measures it

Both camera-based and radar-based launch monitors measure attack angle, but they get there differently.

**Camera-based units** photograph the club through impact. By measuring the head's position across two or more frames just before contact, the software calculates the direction it was traveling.

**Radar-based units** track the club approaching the ball over a brief window before impact. The radar signal tells the unit how the head was moving in the moments leading up to the strike.

**Combined units** use both, blending what each method sees best.

The number is harder to measure with high precision than ball speed or launch angle. Slight differences in how the club is presented can move the reading by a degree or more from one shot to the next, even on what feels like the same swing.

## What it relates to

Attack angle does not act alone. It works together with two other numbers to shape the strike:

- **Dynamic loft.** The actual loft of the face at impact, which can differ from the club's static loft.
- **Spin loft.** The difference between dynamic loft and attack angle. Spin loft helps explain how much the ball spins for a given strike.

For example: a club with high dynamic loft and a steeper (more negative) attack angle creates more spin loft, which generally produces a higher, spinnier ball. A club with a shallower (less negative or positive) attack angle and the same loft produces less spin.

This is why coaches and fitters often look at attack angle as part of a bigger picture of "how is the club being delivered to the ball" rather than as a single number on its own.

## Why your number can vary on the same swing

Like most launch monitor numbers, attack angle can move shot to shot because:

- Where you struck the ball on the face affects the reading.
- Tiny variations in where the ball was relative to your stance change the geometry.
- The condition of the mat or turf affects how the club enters and exits the strike.
- The launch monitor's view of the club may be slightly different from one shot to the next.

A few-degree variation across a session of similar swings is normal. A consistent shift over time, with the same setup, is more meaningful than any single reading.

## What it tells you, in plain English

Attack angle is a description of the club's vertical direction at impact. It is one of several numbers that together describe how the club met the ball. Whether a given attack angle is "good" depends entirely on the club, the player, the desired ball flight, and the kind of strike that produces it. The number itself is just the description.
