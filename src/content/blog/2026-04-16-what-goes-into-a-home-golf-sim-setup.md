A home golf simulator is a small system of parts that work together to do one thing: take a real golf swing in a small space and turn it into a meaningful golf experience. Once you have all the pieces, the whole thing feels like one product. Before you have all the pieces, it can feel like a lot to figure out.

This is a neutral overview of what those pieces are and what each one does. It is not a buying guide and it does not rank brands. It is a map of the components.

## The space

The first decision is the room. Sims need three things from a space: enough length to swing, enough height to swing without hitting the ceiling, and enough width to set up comfortably.

- **Length.** Enough room for the player, the hitting mat, the launch monitor, and the screen, with some room to spare. Short setups can work, but the more length you have, the more flexibility you have with launch monitor placement.
- **Width.** Wide enough for a comfortable stance with a driver, plus space on either side for the camera and the launch monitor.
- **Height.** Tall enough to swing your longest club without hitting the ceiling. The exact number depends on your height and your swing arc. Common sim guides suggest a minimum ceiling height in the 9 to 10 foot range, with more being more comfortable.

Garages, basements, spare bedrooms, and dedicated outbuildings all get used for this. Each comes with its own set of tradeoffs around temperature, light, and noise.

## The hitting surface

A hitting mat is what the ball sits on for each shot. Mats come in many sizes, materials, and thicknesses. The job is to feel reasonable to hit off and to be durable enough that you can hit thousands of balls without destroying it.

Some mats are full mats, large enough to stand on. Some are stance pads with a separate hitting strip. Some have inserts to swap out worn turf. Each setup has its fans.

A few practical considerations:

- Thicker mats absorb more shock, which is easier on the wrists, especially for repeated practice sessions.
- Mats with separate stance and hitting sections let you replace just the hitting part as it wears.
- Mat firmness affects how much the club bounces off the surface compared to a real fairway.

## The screen and the projector (or TV)

You need somewhere to see the simulation, and on most setups, somewhere to absorb the ball.

- **Impact screens** are heavy fabric screens designed to take the ball strike and absorb its energy without bouncing it back. The simulator image is projected onto the front of the screen. The combination of "screen" and "projector image" is the most common setup.
- **Projectors** sit somewhere in the bay (often ceiling-mounted or floor-mounted behind the player) and throw the simulator image onto the impact screen.
- **TVs** can be used instead of a projector if you do not need to hit into the display. The TV sits to the side of the hitting area and the player hits into a separate net.

Each path has tradeoffs around image size, brightness, room geometry, and budget.

## The net (if not hitting into a screen)

If you are not using an impact screen, you need a net to stop the ball. Indoor nets vary widely in size and durability. The job is to absorb the ball safely and let it drop without rebounding too far.

## The launch monitor

The launch monitor is what reads each shot and turns it into numbers. There are camera-based and radar-based units, and a few that combine both. Different monitors sit in different parts of the bay (overhead, behind the player, beside the ball) and have different requirements for room size and light.

Whatever the brand, the launch monitor is the input to the simulator software. The numbers it produces drive what happens on the screen.

## The simulator software

The simulator software takes the launch monitor's numbers and turns them into a game. It loads virtual courses, runs ball-flight physics on top of the player's actual shot data, draws the trees and the wind and the bunkers, and tracks scores.

Many launch monitors have their own software. Many also work with third-party simulator software like GSPro, E6, or TruGolf. Players who use multiple launch monitors often standardize on a single simulator package so the experience is consistent across hardware.

## The computer

Most sim software runs on a Windows PC. A few run on Mac, and a few are integrated into the launch monitor itself. The computer drives the projector or TV, runs the sim software, and (optionally) handles swing video recording and review.

Specs depend on the sim software. Some run on modest machines. Others want a current-generation graphics card. The sim software's documentation is the place to check.

## The camera (or cameras)

Video is optional but most players add at least one camera once they have the rest of the bay working. The two standard angles are down-the-line and face-on. (See [our camera setup post](/blog/how-to-set-up-swing-cameras) for placement details.)

For the camera itself, anything from a built-in laptop webcam to a dedicated high-FPS swing-cam works. The right answer depends on what you want to do with the footage.

## Lighting

Often overlooked. Good light makes everything work better:

- Camera-based launch monitors see the ball more reliably with adequate light.
- Swing video looks dramatically better with even, consistent lighting.
- Projected images on the impact screen look better with controlled light, often less of it.

Striking a balance between "enough light for cameras" and "not so much that the projector image washes out" is part of dialing in a bay. Many players end up with a couple of dedicated lights pointed away from the screen.

## Audio (optional)

Some players add speakers for sim sounds and music. Some leave audio off entirely. Either is fine.

## Putting setup (optional)

Indoor putting setups range from a small mat for tap-ins to a full putting green that takes up a separate area. Some sim software supports putting through the launch monitor. Some uses a separate putting sensor. Some skips putts and uses generous gimme distances.

## Putting it together

A home sim setup tends to grow in stages. Many people start with a launch monitor, a mat, and a net hitting into a TV. They add an impact screen and a projector when they want a real virtual course. They add cameras when they want to see their swing. They add review tools when they want to track progress.

You do not have to do all of it at once. The system is modular by nature. Each piece adds something specific. The best setup is the one that fits your space, your budget, and the parts of the game you actually want to work on.
