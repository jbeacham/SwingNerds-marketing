There are three ways most sim golfers end up filming their swings:

1. A **regular USB webcam** plugged into the bay computer
2. A **phone** acting as a webcam (iPhone via macOS Continuity Camera, or any phone via apps like Camo or EpocCam)
3. A **dedicated golf swing camera** (a high-FPS USB camera built for fast motion, also called a swing cam)

All three work with golf swing camera software like [SwingNerds Vision](/vision). Which one is right for you depends on what you actually need from the video. This is a decision framework.

## The fast answer

If you want to skip the analysis: most people end up with **dedicated swing cams** for their primary down-the-line and face-on angles, and use **phones or webcams** as a starting point or for occasional casual recording.

If you want to understand why, keep reading.

## What each option actually is

### Regular USB webcam

A normal webcam designed for video calls. Examples: Logitech C920, generic Amazon webcams in the $30 to $50 range. Records at 30 to 60 fps, color, rolling shutter, fixed lens. Plugs into the bay computer over USB.

### Phone as a webcam

Recent phones can act as a webcam to a computer:

- **iPhone**: Continuity Camera on macOS (built in, automatic) or Camo / EpocCam on Windows.
- **Android**: Camo, DroidCam, or built-in features in some Android versions.

The phone shows up to your computer as a regular USB camera. The phone's actual camera (which is usually quite good) becomes the video source.

### Dedicated golf swing camera

A USB camera designed specifically for fast motion, often used in industrial vision and sports analysis. Common features: 90 to 240 fps frame rates, **global shutter** sensors, M12 interchangeable lenses, no built-in mic or speaker. Examples: Kayeton, ELP, and similar OEM models in the $80 to $200 range. See [our cameras page](/cameras) for current picks.

## The five dimensions where they differ

### 1. Frame rate

- Webcam: typically 30 fps, sometimes 60 fps. Slow motion looks choppy.
- Phone: 30 fps when used as a webcam. Higher when filming directly on the phone, but that breaks the auto-pairing workflow with golf swing camera software.
- Swing cam: 90 to 240 fps. Smooth slow motion that stays sharp.

**Winner for slow motion**: swing cam, by a wide margin.

### 2. Shutter type (motion clarity)

- Webcam: rolling shutter. Club at impact looks like a wet noodle.
- Phone: rolling shutter. Same issue.
- Swing cam: usually global shutter at this price tier. Club at impact looks like a club.

**Winner for impact clarity**: swing cam, by a wide margin.

### 3. Setup friction

- Webcam: plug in once, mount once, runs forever. Lowest friction.
- Phone: requires the phone to be docked and charging during your session. Apps occasionally drop the connection. The phone is also tied up while it is being used as a camera. You cannot use it for anything else.
- Swing cam: same as a webcam. Plug in once, mount once, runs forever.

**Winner for setup friction**: webcam and swing cam, tied. Phone is more friction.

### 4. Cost

- Webcam: $30 to $50 for one. You may already own one.
- Phone: free if you already have one. The phone is the expensive part, but you have it for other reasons.
- Swing cam: $80 to $200 each, plus optional lens upgrades.

**Winner on cost**: phone if you already have one. Webcam if you have to buy something cheap. Swing cam is the most expensive option for the most capable hardware.

### 5. Image quality (for swing analysis specifically)

- Webcam: fine for "watching your swing." Not enough for studying impact in detail.
- Phone: very good for normal video. Limited for fast motion because of the rolling shutter and the framerate cap when used as a webcam.
- Swing cam: built for this. Sharp at impact, smooth in slow motion, works in dim sim bay lighting.

**Winner**: swing cam for serious analysis. Phone for casual review with great normal-speed footage. Webcam for "I just want to see my swing."

## The decision framework

Match what you want to do to the right tool.

### Use a regular USB webcam if

- You want to start filming immediately with minimal investment
- You mostly care about seeing your swing in real time, not in slow motion
- You are not yet sure how important the video will be to your practice
- You want a permanent always-plugged-in setup at the lowest price

A $30 USB webcam plus golf swing camera software gives you most of the workflow benefit (auto-recording, auto-pairing with shot data, cloud sync) at a price floor that is hard to beat.

### Use your phone as a webcam if

- You already have a recent iPhone or Android and you want zero new hardware
- You want to test the workflow before committing to dedicated equipment
- You only sim a few times a month and the phone is not in active use during that time

The image quality from a recent phone is genuinely better than a $30 webcam. The catch is the friction of docking and dedicating the phone to recording for the duration of your session.

### Use a dedicated golf swing camera if

- You want to actually study your swing in slow motion frame by frame
- You care about seeing impact clearly (face strike, club path, ball at the moment of contact)
- You have the budget for $100 to $200 of camera per angle
- You want the camera permanently mounted in the bay so it just runs every session

This is the upgrade most people land on long-term. The combination of high FPS, global shutter, and an interchangeable lens makes a real difference in what you can see.

## A common path

Many sim golfers go through this progression naturally:

1. **Start with a phone or laptop webcam** to confirm the workflow is useful (auto-recording, auto-pairing, side-by-side comparison).
2. **Add a $30 USB webcam** mounted in the bay so it runs without setup each session.
3. **Upgrade one angle to a dedicated swing cam** (usually down-the-line first, since that is where impact gets studied).
4. **Add a second swing cam** for the face-on angle.

This path lets you commit gradually as you confirm that swing video is actually changing how you practice. If it turns out you only check video occasionally, you can stop at step 1 or 2 and not feel bad about it.

## Mixing is fine

You do not have to pick one option and stick with it for every angle. A common setup we see: a 120 fps swing cam for the down-the-line angle (where you study impact) and a phone or webcam for face-on (where the slow motion matters less and the better natural-looking image of a phone is nice).

## What none of them replace

Whatever camera you pick, the camera is just one part of the system. You also need:

- **Mounting** that holds the camera steady at the right height and angle. (See [our camera setup guide](/blog/how-to-set-up-swing-cameras) for positions.)
- **Lighting** so the camera has enough light to do its job at high frame rates.
- **Software** that records, pairs, and stores the video. The whole point of [SwingNerds Vision](/vision).

The camera is the front of the funnel. Good software makes whatever camera you have more useful. The wrong camera makes good software work harder than it should. Match the camera to your goals and the rest of the system follows.
