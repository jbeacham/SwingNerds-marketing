Almost every launch monitor on the market today uses one of two underlying technologies: high-speed cameras or Doppler radar. A few combine both. The two approaches see a golf shot in completely different ways, which is why they tend to live in different settings.

This is a description of how each one works, not a ranking.

## Camera-based (photometric)

A camera-based launch monitor works the way it sounds. One or more very high-speed cameras photograph the golf ball and the club at and immediately after impact. The cameras run at thousands of frames per second, capturing the ball in two or more positions just after it leaves the face.

By comparing those images, the software can measure:

- How fast the ball moved between frames (ball speed)
- The angle it left the face at (launch angle)
- Whether it left the face left or right of straight (launch direction)
- How fast it was rotating, often by tracking dimples or applied dots on the ball (spin rate and spin axis)

For club data, a separate camera or the same set of cameras photograph the club through impact. The software measures the head's position and orientation across frames to derive club speed, attack angle, club path, and face angle.

Because cameras need light to see, most photometric units are designed for indoor use, often with their own integrated lighting. They typically sit overhead or just to the side of the hitting area.

## Radar-based (Doppler)

A Doppler radar launch monitor works on the same principle a police speed gun does. It emits a radar signal, and when that signal bounces off something moving (the ball, the club), the returning signal comes back at a slightly different frequency. The size of that frequency shift tells the device how fast the object is moving and in what direction.

A modern radar launch monitor does this thousands of times a second across the ball's flight, building a continuous track. From that track, it works out:

- How fast the ball was launched
- Its launch angle and direction
- Its spin rate (by detecting tiny variations in the bounced signal as the ball rotates)
- Its full ball-flight curve, including apex and landing point

For club data, the unit picks up the head approaching the ball, then uses the strike itself as the reference point. From a few milliseconds before and after impact, it works out club speed, attack angle, and path.

Because radar is tracking the ball through actual flight, the unit needs that flight to actually happen. Radar units typically sit several feet behind the hitter and want some open space in front, though many can also work indoors against a screen by extrapolating from the early part of the flight.

## What each kind tends to see well

Both kinds output the same list of numbers. They get there differently, and each method has things it sees more directly than the other.

- **Cameras** measure the ball at and just after the strike, so club data and impact dynamics (face, path, attack) are observed directly at the source. They typically need a marked or dotted ball to nail down spin precisely.
- **Radar** observes the ball through a long arc of flight. That makes full-flight curves and spin behavior over distance very natural to track. Club data is derived from a shorter window around impact.

Different manufacturers also combine the two. A combined unit might use cameras for impact and club data, and radar for ball flight, blending the strengths of each.

## Where each tends to live

It is not a hard rule, but in practice:

- **Indoor sim bays** lean toward camera-based units. They are compact, fit overhead or in a small footprint, and do not need open ball flight to work well.
- **Outdoor ranges and tour fitting bays** lean toward radar units. They thrive on open distance and are easy to move from spot to spot.

Plenty of bays use radar indoors, plenty of fitters use cameras outdoors, and plenty of products do both. The "this kind goes here" idea is just a tendency, not a requirement.

## What it means for your data

For the data you eventually look at on a screen, the underlying technology mostly does not matter. The numbers come out labeled the same way: ball speed, launch angle, spin rate, club path, and so on.

Where the technology shows up is in how the unit is set up, how much room it needs, what light it requires, and whether it needs you to put a sticker on the ball. Once the shot is recorded, the data downstream looks the same.
