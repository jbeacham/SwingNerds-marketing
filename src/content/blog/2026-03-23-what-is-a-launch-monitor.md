A launch monitor is a device that watches a golf shot and turns it into numbers. That is the entire job. Where a stopwatch turns a sprint into a single number, a launch monitor turns a single golf shot into ten or twenty.

The numbers fall into two groups: what the ball does, and what the club does.

## What the ball does

The ball half is the headline data. It is what most people think about when they think about a launch monitor.

- **Ball speed.** How fast the ball is moving the moment it leaves the face.
- **Launch angle.** The vertical angle the ball leaves the face at.
- **Launch direction.** The horizontal angle. Left or right of the target line.
- **Spin rate.** How fast the ball is rotating, usually in revolutions per minute.
- **Spin axis.** Whether the ball is spinning on a tilted axis, which is what creates a draw or a fade.

From those, the device calculates **carry distance**, **total distance**, **apex height**, and **side carry** (how far offline the ball lands). Those are not measured directly. They are predictions, run through a ball-flight model.

## What the club does

The club half is harder to measure and not every device captures all of it. The common ones are:

- **Club head speed.** How fast the head is moving at impact.
- **Smash factor.** Ball speed divided by club speed. A measure of how cleanly the ball was struck.
- **Attack angle.** Whether the club was moving down, level, or up at impact.
- **Club path.** The direction the head was moving at impact, relative to the target line.
- **Face angle.** Where the face was pointed at impact.

Those last three together explain why the ball started where it started and why it curved the way it curved.

## How it actually measures all of this

There are two main ways a launch monitor sees a shot.

**Camera-based** (sometimes called photometric). One or more very high-speed cameras photograph the ball and club at the moment of impact. The software measures the ball and club between frames and calculates the rest. These tend to live indoors, looking down at the hitting area.

**Radar-based** (Doppler radar). The device sends out a radar signal and watches how the signal bounces off the ball as it flies. By tracking the ball over distance, it works out how fast it was launched, how much it is spinning, and where it is going. These tend to sit behind the player and need room for the ball to actually fly.

Each approach has its strengths. We will get into the differences in a separate post. The thing to know is that whichever kind you have, the output looks the same: a list of numbers per shot.

## Where the numbers come from on a sim

If you are hitting in a simulator, the launch monitor's numbers are the input. The simulator software (GSPro, E6, TruGolf, and others) takes those numbers and runs them through a ball-flight model on a virtual course. The trees, the wind, the lie, and the bounce are all simulated on top of what the launch monitor measured.

In other words: the launch monitor is the part that watches your real swing. Everything you see on the screen after that is software.

## So what is it for

A launch monitor turns a shot you can feel into numbers you can compare. Your last 7-iron versus the one before. Your range session today versus your range session last month. Your ball speed at the start of the year versus the end of the year.

Without one, you have what your eyes saw and what the ball did. With one, you have a number for every part of it. That is the entire pitch.
