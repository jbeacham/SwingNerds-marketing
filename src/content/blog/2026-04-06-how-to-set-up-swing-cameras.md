A surprising amount of what looks like "my swing video looks weird" is actually "my camera is in the wrong place." Once the camera is in the right spot, the same swing tells you a lot more.

This is a plain-English guide to the two standard angles that almost every coach and almost every swing reviewer uses: down-the-line and face-on. The conventions are not arbitrary. They are placed where they reveal the most about the swing.

## Down-the-line (DTL)

Down-the-line means the camera is looking down the target line, behind the ball, with the golfer between the camera and the target.

For a right-handed golfer, that means the camera sits behind the ball, on the target line, behind the player's heels. The lens is pointed past the player toward the target.

### Position

- Place the camera so its lens is **on the target line**, directly behind the ball.
- For a right-handed player, the camera ends up roughly behind the back of the player. For a left-handed player, mirror it.
- The camera is far enough back that you can see the full club arc on the backswing and follow-through, plus a comfortable amount of headroom and footroom. Six to eight feet behind the ball is a common starting point.

### Height

Hand height. Specifically, **even with the player's hands at address.** That usually puts the lens somewhere between belt buckle and mid-thigh.

This is the convention because it gives the most accurate read on the shaft plane. If the camera is too high, the shaft looks flatter than it is. Too low, it looks more upright. At hand height, what you see is closer to what is actually happening.

### Angle

Level. The camera should not be tilted up or down. If you have to tilt it to fit the swing in frame, move the camera back instead.

## Face-on

Face-on means the camera is looking directly at the front of the player, perpendicular to the target line.

### Position

- Place the camera **perpendicular to the target line**, on the side of the ball that the player is facing. For a right-handed player swinging toward a target on the left of frame, that means the camera is in front of the player.
- Center it on the ball, not on the player's body.
- Distance is similar to DTL. Far enough back that the full swing arc fits comfortably in frame.

### Height

Hand height, same as DTL. Belt buckle to mid-thigh works for most setups.

This matters even more for face-on than for DTL. A face-on camera that is too high makes the player look like they are reverse pivoting. Too low and you lose the relationship between the hands, the chest, and the ball.

### Angle

Level. Pointed straight at the ball, not tilted.

## Why both angles

Each angle hides different things.

- DTL is the place to look at swing plane, club path, and how the shaft moves through impact.
- Face-on is the place to look at posture, sequencing, weight shift, and where the low point of the swing actually is.

When something looks wrong on one angle and fine on the other, that is information. When both angles agree, you have a good chance of seeing the actual issue.

## Common mistakes

- **Camera too high.** Almost everyone does this the first time, because phone tripods are usually set up around eye level. Bring it down to hand height.
- **DTL camera off the target line.** If the camera is even a foot to the left or right of the line, the shaft plane in the video is misleading. Take a moment to actually line it up.
- **Lens not centered on the ball.** People center the camera on the player. Center it on the ball. The ball is what matters.
- **Tripod too short or too tall, so they tilt the camera.** Tilting the camera distorts what you see. Move the camera, do not angle it.

## A simple alignment trick

Stand at address with a club. Your hands are now where the camera should be aimed at, vertically. Step out of the way. Whatever the camera was looking at last is the height it should stay at.

For DTL, lay a club or alignment stick on the ground from the ball pointing straight back along the target line, then position the camera directly above the end of that stick. Now you know it is on the line.

## You do not need fancy gear to start

Almost any camera will work for getting your positions right and seeing what your swing actually looks like.

- A built-in laptop webcam is fine for a first pass.
- A phone, especially an iPhone, can be used as a webcam through Continuity Camera (built into recent macOS) or third-party apps like Camo or EpocCam, which let your phone show up to a Windows or Mac as a regular USB camera.
- A $30 USB webcam from Amazon will do the job too.

The reason this matters: a lot of the value of a swing camera is just seeing what your body is doing from the outside. You do not need 240 frames per second to learn that your DTL camera is too high or that your weight is hanging back. Get the positions right with whatever you have, and once you have a workflow you actually use, then think about upgrading the camera.
