Of all the numbers a launch monitor reports, smash factor is the one with the simplest definition.

## The formula

> Smash factor = ball speed &divide; club head speed

That is it. Both numbers are measured at the moment of impact, both in the same unit (usually miles per hour or kilometers per hour). Divide one by the other and you have smash factor.

If your club head is moving 100 mph and the ball comes off at 145 mph, your smash factor is 1.45.

## What the number represents

Smash factor is a measure of energy transfer from the club to the ball. It is the ratio of how fast the ball ends up moving compared to how fast the club was moving when it hit it.

A higher number means more of the club's speed was converted into ball speed. A lower number means less.

Three things move the number:

- **Where on the face you struck the ball.** The center of the face transfers the most energy. The toe, heel, top, and bottom transfer less.
- **The angle of attack and dynamic loft.** The relationship between how the club is moving and how the face is presented at impact affects how much of the club's speed becomes forward ball speed.
- **The club itself.** A driver, with its long shaft and large flat face, will produce a different smash factor than a wedge with a lofted face that is designed to put spin on the ball rather than maximum speed.

## A quick example

Two shots, same swing speed, different contact:

| | Club speed | Ball speed | Smash |
|---|---|---|---|
| Center strike | 95 mph | 142 mph | 1.49 |
| Off the toe | 95 mph | 130 mph | 1.37 |

Same swing. Different smash factor. The club did the same thing in both cases. The ball did not.

## Why driver smash and wedge smash are not the same

Because the formula divides ball speed by club speed, the design of the club matters.

A driver is built to send the ball forward with very little spin. Almost all of the club's energy goes into ball speed. The number you see is high.

A wedge is built to put loft and spin on a ball that is sitting on the ground. A lot of the energy goes into making the ball climb and spin, not into making it fly forward fast. The number you see is lower.

Comparing your driver smash to your wedge smash is not really a comparison. They are different jobs.

## What it does not tell you

Smash factor is one number that summarizes one thing: how cleanly the ball came off the face for that club. It does not say whether the ball started on line. It does not say whether it spun the right amount. It does not say whether it carried the right distance. Those are other numbers.

It is a useful single number to glance at, club by club, to get a sense of how clean your contact was. That is all.
